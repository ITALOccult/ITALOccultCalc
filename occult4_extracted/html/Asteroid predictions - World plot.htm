Asteroid/Planet occultation - Main prediction

This form is the principal form for displaying an occultation prediction, and accessing more detailed predictions.

Moving between plots

  • You can step forwards or backwards through the list of events displayed in List and Display simply by using the buttons with the '⇦' and '⇨', or the items under the menu item Move to.... And you can click Exit, or close the form, to cease moving through the file.

  •  

  • Earth map

  • The map of the Earth is drawn from the direction of the star. The sunlit region is indicated by light-pink circles. The line corresponding to sunset (solar zenith distance of 90° 50') is marked with a thick orange line. The three dashed orange lines correspond to Civil Twilight (-6°), Nautical Twilight (-12°) and Astronomical Twilight (-18°).
  • The map of the Earth is oriented in accordance with the time of closest approach of the path to the nominated plot site - which is plotted as a blue '+'. The coordinates of this location are set just above the plot at the boxes for Site Longitude and latitude. When a map is first plotted, the site set in the Selection Criteria is inserted as the site location. You can reset the site location by double-clicking on a location, followed by the button with the '||'.
  •  Note: plotting the map on the basis of the site location is very important for two issues
    1. for slow-moving occultations, the path may extend well beyond a hemisphere; you need to select a plot site near the path to bring the path onto the 'visible' side of the Earth;
    2. Similarly, for slow-moving events the location of the terminator is dependent on the time used for the plot. So to get the correct positioning of the terminator and twilight zones, you need to select a plot site near the position along the path of interest.
    1. For very slow events (lasting hours, or even over a day: if the duration to cross the Earth is greater than 1 hour, the Map will be draw with the longitude of the site on the central meridian of the world plot.
    2. For shorter events, the basis of the plot can be swapped using the radio button 'Time Centered' in the menu bar at the top of the form.
  •  The occultation path on the earth is plotted as two solid lines (the northern and southern limit), and two dotted lines (the location of the limits assuming a 1-sigma displacement of the occultation path. The path location takes into account the Earth's rotation. For slow events the path can appear quite distorted; it can also extend beyond the displayed hemisphere (indeed, can even extend more than 360deg in longitude), or (more rarely) only extend part way across a displayed hemisphere. Time markers are given at 1 minute intervals (for plots greater than a scale of 1). 5 and 10 minute markers are of greater length.
  •  The occultation path 'in space' is also shown, terminating at the first minute before/after the occultation. Where there appears to be a lateral displacement between the path on the Earth, and that 'in space', that is a plotting effect arising from the earth's rotation.
  •  The plot can be drawn at different scales, using the Plot scale buttons at the top left. The scales vary from 0.1 to 32 (but the Redraw menu item has an additional scale of 0.01). The button with || merely redraws at the same scale. When the occultation path misses the earth, the scale of '1' is automatically adjusted on the basis of the minimum distance of the path from the center of the Earth.
  •  You can plot sites, and site names, using the drop-down boxes at top right (after disabling the Export buttons from the Menu item Options... Export buttons). The left box enables you to select a site file. The right box set the criteria for including site names on the plot (using the settings in the site file for each site.)

  • Uncertainty ellipse, and star chart

  • An uncertainty ellipse is plotted, showing the 1-sigma uncertainty ellipse. It can be re-positioned to the location of the cursor with a left click. [If the position is over the map, you will have to follow the left click by clicking the replot button with the '||'.] You can choose to not plot the ellipse, using the relevant menu item under Options....
  • A small star chart (2 x 2 deg) is displayed. The occulted star is always in the exact center, surrounded by a grey circle. Where a star has a Bayer letter or Flamsteed number, that is included. The star chart shows the motion of the asteroid by way of a green dashed line. The end of each dash corresponds to 0hrs UT. The line has 5 dashes before the occultation, and one dash afterwards. The line is drawn such that there is no dash on the day of the occultation. You can choose to not plot the star chart, using the relevant menu item under Options....

  • Maps of satellite and ring orbits

    The presence of moons or rings will be noted at the top of the plot, with whatever information is available.

    Where an asteroid has one or more satellites with sufficiently characterized orbits (radius and orientation), Occult can plot a representation of the satellite system Similarly when an asteroid has a ring system that is similarly characterized. The plot is only included if the menu item 'display asteroid's ring and moon systems' is selected.

    The plots are oriented as a projection onto the Earth, and are compatible with the occultation path as plotted. [The directions N, E and W arev indicated on the plots.] However these plots are not drawn at the same scale as the earth - and the length of the sides of  each plot is given at the bottom of each plot.     When the apparent orbit appears elliptic, the expected times of occultation by the rings are not symmetrical about the time for the main body.     Issues when the star's diameter is significant

    When the star has a significant diameter, the circumstances of the occultation will be affected. The main effect is that the light curves will have a degree of being gradual. However it is possible for an occultation to be an annular event.

    Where the effect of stellar diameter is significant, a light curve will be included in the prediction plot.

    The usual path limits of the occultation are actually the limits of a partial occultation (equivalent to the penumbral limits for a solar eclipse). When the star diameter is significant, the plot will also include lines for the region where the occultation is a 'total' occultation, or an 'annular' occultation. These lines will similarly be included in GoogleEarth plots of the event.

    In this plot, the Magenta dashed lines near the center of the path indicate the path where the occultation is annular. The small light curve plot shows the expected light level during the occultation. The scale on the left side is brightness level, on a scale of 0 to 1. The scale on the right side gives the magnitude drop (values of 1, 2 & 3 are shown.)

    Long-duration events

    If an event occurs near the stationary point in the asteroid's apparent motion, the path on the Earth's surface can become quite complex. It is possible for the path to extend around the earth multiple times. To ensure the path location is accurately predicted for these events, the occultation elements are computed using positions of the asteroid calculated at a time interval commensurate with the total event duration (4 positions at step intervals of 2.5, 5, 10, or 20 hours[depending on the overall event duration], rather than the usual step of 1 hour).

    Header information

  • The top line gives the asteroid/object, star identifier, date, and time range during which the occultation occurs somewhere on the earth. When the event extends over a day boundary, the date here is always set to the day on which the event starts, with the end time being an hour greater that 24 hours.
  • At the left, details of the star. Magnitudes v (Visual) and r (Red). Coordinates are J2000, epoch of date. If a correction is applied to the relative position of the asteroid/planet and star, the correction will appear as a change in the star's position. Note that the precision of the displayed coordinates is generally less than the precision used when the occultation elements were generated. A reliability value is displayed. Usually it is the Gaia RUWE value. However under certain circumstances a warning is displayed. One of those is for stars where the position is from the 'old' HIP2 catalogue, with the possibility that it is a low-precision duplicate of another star in the Gaia catalogue
  • Also at top left - the date on which the occultation elements were generated. [This allows people to assess whether the prediction is 'the latest'.]
  • At top center there are details of certain circumstances, including details of the moon phase and elongation. Where possible, the estimated magnitude drop is given in visual. If the Red magnitude of an asteroid is available (see next dot point) the estimated drop in Red is given. For both, the equivalent % drop is given in brackets). Also, the parameters of the uncertainty ellipse are given - with the axis numbers being the semi-axes of the ellipse.
  • At top right - details of the asteroid, including both the physical and apparent diameter, and the hourly rate of motion. If the asteroid has 2-color parameters in the file HGsHG1G2.csv, both Visual and Red magnitudes will be listed. Otherwise only the Visual magnitude. If the asteroid is contained in the ISAM database of asteroid light curve inversion models, (in ISAM) will appear after the label Asteroid.
  • At  bottom right, the basis of the prediction is given. The first component is usually the source of the asteroid position, the 2nd is the basis of the errors. Where the source of the asteroid position is from JPL horizons, the Orbit solution identifier in Horizons is included. The word Ephemeris appears if the final solution is based on the Horizons ephemeris of the asteroid (rather than Occult generating positions from orbital elements). It indicates the prediction is of the highest level of accuracy.

  • Footer information

    At the bottom left of the line that gives the following information

    • The version of Occult used to generate the plot
    • the source and date of the orbital elements used for the asteroid
    • the basis of the errors in the prediction. In particular, whether they are 'measured' values, or assumed values
    • if the objects orbit has not been integrated, that fact (applies mainly to comets)
    • the taxonomic class (other than a main belt asteroid) (click here for the classes displayed).

    Advisory messages

    At the top right of the plot, immediately under the header, an advisory message can appear indicating one or more of the following:
  • The prediction has come from the LuckyStar or RIO programs
  • The taxonomic class of the asteroid (The classes that can be displayed are listed here)
  • The asteroid is listed as a Binary Asteroid candidate on the basis of analysis of Gaia FDR3 astrometry of asteroids. The data shown is the mean orbital separation in km (with uncertainty), the estimated diameter, and the estimated maximum duration. There are always 2 solutions shown. If the separation is greater than 400km, the distance is given as a '?'. For some these, the separations can be over 20,000km. The potential distance in path widths can be determined by dividing the distance by the usual asteroid diameter as listed in the event header.
  • The asteroid is known to have one or more moons - with basic details about those moons
  • The star is listed in the Washington Double Star catalog, or indicated as double in other catalogues, or is in the AAVSO Index file of Variable stars.

    Optional control buttons

  • In the menu line there are 5 icons to click on to generate:
    • a plot in Google Earth
    • the path coordinates
    • a star chart 
    • a list of pre-point stars
    • for planetary events, a plot of the planet's configuration

  • The boxes used to control the plotting of sites can be replaced with a set of 'Export buttons' using a setting in the User settings. These buttons allow you to move through a large file of occultation elements, and copy particular occultation elements to any of 7 files. By this means you can create subset files limited to events visible in a particular region. When you save the elements to a particular subset file, the button is disabled.
    The subset files have the same path and file name as the occultation elements file - but have ".Export A" through to "G" appended after the extension. The subset files can be read in the same manner as the original occultation element file.

Mutual eclipses and occultations of satellites

This module computes predictions of mutual eclipses and occultations of the major satellites of Jupiter, Saturn, and Uranus. Eclipses occur in a well-defined period as the sun passes through the orbital plane of the satellites. However, the Earth passes through the orbital plane either once, or three times, at each apparition. As a result, occultations can occur either over an extended period.

For satellites whose orbits are close to co-planar, eclipses and occultations occur when the Sun or Earth passes through the orbital plane. When the orbital planes are not close to being co-planar, mutual events only occur when the respective satellites are at the location of the node of the apparent orbits. There are two such satellites:

  • Iapetus (Saturn). The orbit is inclined 15 deg. to the orbits of the other satellites. Its orbit is outside the orbits of the other satellites. The result is that eclipses and occultations do not occur when the Earth/Sun passes through the orbital plane of the other satellites, but several years removed. The number of eclipses and occultations that occur during such an apparition is quite small. In the 2006/7 apparition, there is one such event - on 2007 Jan 6, Iapetus occults Mimas. To make sure events are found, specify a large minimum separation, such as 4”.
  • Miranda (Uranus). The orbit is inclined 4.3 deg. to the orbits of the other satellites. Its orbit is inside the orbits of the other major satellites of Uranus. The result is that eclipses and occultations occur over a period centered on when the Earth/Sun passes through the orbital plane of the other satellites, but occur over an extended period. However, the frequency of events is reduced as a result of the significant inclination of the orbit.

The Earth/Sun passes through the orbital planes of the major satellites every 6 years for Jupiter, 14 years for Saturn and 42 years for Uranus. The events occur over a period of around 15 months for Jupiter, 2 years for Saturn (less for Iapetus), and 6 months for Uranus (somewhat longer for events involving Miranda). The planet for the predictions is selected using the radio buttons at the top left. The search is conducted on the basis of a start year and month, and an end year and month. For the end date, the date is taken as the last day of the specified month. The predictions can be limited to events that are visible at the specified site location. That is, the planet above the horizon, and the sun at least 6 degrees below the horizon. Events where the satellites come close but do not eclipse or transit, can be included by selecting a "miss distance" greater than 0". [For most purposes a miss distance of 0" should be selected.]

The events found in the search are displayed in two boxes. The left box gives the details of all events found. The right box gives the contact times for any highlighted event. A typical prediction line (with the header line) is as follows. It involves satellites of Jupiter.

    Viewing the Planet and its satellites

    To get a visual impression of the location of the satellites relative to the planet and other satellites, select an event in the list, then double-click on that event. This will bring up the usual graphic of the planet and its satellites.

    Graphic displays

    1. Light curves

    Next to the contact times, the form displays:

    • the expected light curve for the event (upper plot). This light curve fully takes into account changes in the relative rate of motion of the wto satellites, or the sattelite and shadow. The light curve plot always starts at an integral minute; as a result the distribution is generally not centered within the plot.
    • if the event is an eclipse, the light distribution across the eclipse shadow (lower plot). The shadow region is shown with a light-yellow background, with the black curve representing the light level.
    The following example is of the 2015 March 8 III ecl IV event.:

    The magnitude of the central eclipse - as seen from the distance of the eclipsed satellite - is given at the bottom of the plot. Most eclipses have a central umbral region, with the magnitude of the eclipse being between 1.0 and about 4.5. For eclipses involving Ganymede or Callisto it is possible for the shadow to be entirely penumbral; such eclipses are annular as seen from the distance of the eclipsed satellite, with a magnitude between about 0.7 and 1

    A larger plot of the light curve can be generated by either double-clicking on the upper plot, or from the menu item with Predictions... Display light curve (Large). This large plot is designed to overlay an observed light curve - so that observers can get a feel about how close their observation matches prediction.

    2. Time-representation of the event

    In the lower portion of the form is a graphic display of the circumstances of the mutual event. The display shows the location of the occulting moon/eclipse-shadow relative to the occulted or eclipsed moon. The circumstances are plotted at 11 equally spaced points in time, centred on the mid-time of the event.

    For an occultation the the occulting satellite is shown in a lightl range, while the occulted satellite is in yellow.     In the plot:

  • The occulted or eclipsed satellite is plotted on the horizontal line, and is coloured yellow;
  • The occulting satellite, or the eclipse shadow, is plotted relative to the occulted or eclipsed satellite - with a dotted line joining them
  • For occultations, the occulting moon is shown as a full circle and is coloured orange, with the occulted satellite being faintly visible behind it.
  • For eclipses, the shadow is shown with umbra (brown circle) and penumbra (grey region). For smaller satellites the shadow may have no umbral region.
  • The UT of each item is displayed below the occulted/eclipsed satellite.

    The time interval of the plot is based on the predicted duration of the event. The time span can be increased using the option buttons in the box labeled Increase plot time-span. This is particularly useful for events where the two moons (or moon + shadow) are moving largely in the same direction, such that the relative motion is very non-linear. An example is the 2015 May 21 III ecl I event. The list of events provides two events, with mid-times at 6h 51m and 8h 6m. By selecting Increase plot Time-span to x2, you can visualise the entire event - which shows the shadow of Ganymede passing eastwards across Europa, and then reversing direction and passing westwards across Europa, this time with a greater minimum separation.

    The light curve neatly shows this extended event with two minima.

    The scale of the plot is set according to whether the event involves Jupiter, Saturn or Uranus. This scale can be increased using the option buttons in the box labeled Increase plot scale. For occultations, the scale is the angular distance as seen from the Earth. For eclipses, the scale is the angular distance as seen from the Sun; in the case of Jupiter, this can differ from the geocentric scale by up to 20%.

    Technical notes

    The routine generates the prediction by computing the separation at two times separated by 6 minutes - and assumes linear motion over this time. The routine iterates for the time of closest approach. The assumption of linear motion is less valid as the duration of the event increases; events lasting more than a few minutes arise only in circumstances when the motion of the inner satellite of the pair is significantly non-linear. For some events the iteration does not converge. Such events are marked by an asterisk, and arise when the relative motion of the moons is very small.

    For occultations, the maximum possible magnitude drop is 0.75 mag - assuming both objects are of the same size, have the same albedo, and the occultation is central. The predicted magnitude drop takes into account the different sizes and albedos of the satellites involved.

    For eclipses the magnitude drop is a combination of the area of the eclipsed moon that is covered by the shadow, and the brightnes distribution across the shadow. The brightness distribution is based on the degree of obscuration of the limb-darkened sun by the eclipsing satellite as seen from the eclipsed satellite. The area of the moon covered by the shadow is calculated by dividing the shadow into 100 annuli of equal width (with each annulus having a brightness level depending in the location within the shadow), and computing the area of the moon that is covered by each annulus and its corresponding brightness level), and adding the results.

    The brightness of each annulus in the shadow is calculated by dividing the sun into 100 annuli having a brightness proportioned according to solar limb darkening, and adding the contribution of each annulus depending on their visibility at the mid-point of each shadow annulus.

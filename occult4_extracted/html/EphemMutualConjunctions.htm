Mutual conjunctions (appulses) of the planets

This option provides predictions of mutual conjunctions of the major planets. The search separation can be up to 200 arcmins. Each event can be displayed graphically by selecting the event.

    Note: The term conjunction is frequently used together with in right ascension, or in longitude - to indicate when two bodies have the same right ascension or ecliptic longitude. The time when two bodies are at closest approach (appulse) usually differs from the time of conjunction in right ascension, or in ecliptic longitude.

The prediction gives;

  • The date and Universal time of closest approach (in decimal hours).;
  • The separation of the centers of the planets as seen from the geocenter at closest approach, in arc minutes or arcsecs;
  • The separation of the limbs of the planets as seen from the edge of the Earth where the planets are closest. geocenter at closest approach, in arc minutes or arcsecs. A -'ve value indicates that one planet overlies part or all of the other;
  • The type of event.
  • Total = one planet either wholly hides the other, or one is wholly within the other - as seen from somewhere on the Earth
  • Part = one planet partially hides the other - as seen from somewhere on the Earth
  • Miss = no planet partially hides the other as seen from anywhere on the Earth.
  •  The elongation of the planets from the Sun (and whether the elongation is East or West); and
  • The planets involved, together with their magnitude..

    The graphic of the event shows the two planets, as illustrated in the following event (which is the next planet/planet occultation to occur.)/p>

    The initial plot scale is set by the minimum separation given on the initial prediction. Change the scale for the plot as required using the option buttons at the left. The scale is the vertical height of the plot screen (in minutes of arc.) The plot is drawn with North to the top, East to the left. It shows:

    • The identity of the planet closest to the Earth - as the first planet named in the title on the plot.
    • In the center, the planet furthest from the Earth.
    • The planet closer to the Earth, plotted relative to the other planet at 1-hour intervals over a period of 5 hours;
    • If one of the planets is Jupiter - the location of the Galilean satellites, plotted at hourly intervals. The satellite at the start of the sequence is drawn larger than for the other positions. If Jupiter is not in the centre, the satellite positions are plotted with respect to the plotted position of Jupiter.
    • If one of the planets is Saturn - the location of Titan, and the rings. If Saturn is in the centre, Titan is shown at hourly intervals. If Saturn is not in the centre, the position of Titan is plotted with respect to each plotted position of the Saturn. Titan is shown larger at the start of the sequence than for the other positions.
    • If one of the planets is Mercury or Venus, its phase.
    • A plot of the Earth, showing the hemisphere from which the planets are visible at the time of closest approach. [The brown circles indicate the sunlit portion of the Earth.]

    Historically there has only ever been one recorded mutual occultation - an occultation of Mercury by Venus observed from Greenwich Observatory on 1737 May 28, by John Bevis. The original report (which is in 'scholarly latin') was published in Philosophical Transactions, Vol 41 (1739-1741) pp 630-632. Bevis was an amateur astronomer who had access to Greenwich Observatory. While the planets were at a solar elongation of 21.7 deg, the altitude at the time of the event was only 1.4 deg, and there were issues of scattered cloud. Computations in Occult using the JPL-DE431 ephemeris show that Mercury was never completely covered by Venus. The event is discussed by Roger Sinnott and Jean Meeus in Sky and Telescope, September 1986, p220-222.

    Another historical event of interest is the occultation of Neptune by Jupiter on 1613 Jan 3. Whilst that event was not observed, it turns out that drawings of the Jovian system made by Galileo on 28 December and 28 January showed a 'star' which was in fact Neptune. [See Nature Vol 287 25 Sept 1980, page 311 for full details.]

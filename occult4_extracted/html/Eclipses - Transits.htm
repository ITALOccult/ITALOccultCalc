Transits

The form for transit predictions has a list at the left of all possible transits of Mercury and Venus that occur in a 100-year period. The 100-year period is set by changing the year above the list (and pressing Enter). The available range of years is -13000 to +17000 - corresponding to the coverage of the DE431 ephemeris. To display a prediction for a transit, simply select the transit in the 100-year list.

Notes

  • Any '+' and '-' signs after the event time indicate the event occurs in the day after or before (respectively) the date specified at the top of the prediction.
  • Two maps of the world show the Earth as seen from the Sun. The first is for the time of geocentric 1st contact. The second is for the time of geocentric 4th contact. The relevant times are shown above each map. They show the regions from where the transit will be visible, and are drawn on the basis of the estimated value of deltaT (even if the predicted times are in TT). The uncertainty in the Earth's orientation is given at the bottom of the plot (when the uncertainty in deltaT is greater than 2 secs.)
  • When there is no transit, each map is identical, being drawn for the time of closest approach of the planet to the sun.
  • The other image represents the path of the planet during the transit. Note that the planet ALWAYS crosses the Sun from East to West.
  • The minimum geocentric separation, and the apparent radii of the Sun and the relevant planet, are given in arc secs.
  • The ephemeris used for the prediction is given in the line specifying the value of deltaT.
  • Times are given in UT provided the uncertainty in deltaT is less than 1 hour (ie between -1970 and +3471). Otherwise the time are in TT.

Grazing transits

On rare occasions a transit may be a grazing transit. Recent examples are:

  • Mercury in 1999 November 15, which was a full transit for the northern hemisphere, but a partial transit in southern Australia;
  • Mercury in 1937 May 11, which was a partial transit in Africa and southern Asia, but otherwise a complete miss.

When a transit is a grazing transit, the prediction includes a line to indicate the nature of the event, as in:

   {'+' => next day; '-' => previous day }

Also provided in a separate table is a set of latitude/longitude coordinates of the limit line between the Total/Partial phase, or between the Partial phase and a Miss. The uncertainty in the rotational orientation of the earth is given at the end of the table, when it is 0.1 deg or greater.

The next grazing transit of Mercury will be on 2391 May 11 [although transits that are close to grazing occur in 2154, 2174, 2210, 2371, and 2387.] The previous grazing transit of Mercury occurred on 1342 October 21. A grazing transit of Venus last occurred on 1631 Dec 7; the next will be on 2611 December 13.

For grazing and near-grazing transits, the contact times will be very dependent upon the location of the site. Where a grazing transit occurs, the line dividing the zone of partial transit from a total transit is drawn in blue; that between a partial transit and a miss is drawn in red. In theory it is possible for both lines to exist at a May transit of Mercury, but not for a November transit of Mercury, or a transit of Venus. For some transits of Venus, the transit is partial all over the Earth. The type of the transit [total/partial, or partial/miss] is indicated, together with the limit line coordinates at 5-degree intervals, in a separate box.

Local predictions

Local predictions can be generated for a range of sites. Merely select a Site file from the drop-down box, and click Multisite predictions using.... Details. However this ability is disabled when the uncertainty in deltaT is greater than 60 minutes (ie before -1970, and after 3471 ).

Transit timings and analyses

A listing of contact timings for transits of Mercury for the period 1677-1973 is given in Royal Greenwich Observatory Bulletin No.181, 359-420 (1975). Unfortunately this article is not available on-line.

An analyses of past transits of Mercury is published in the MNRAS

An analysis of (non-)variations in the solar diameter using transist of mercury is published in Nature, 288, 548-551 (1980).

Light curve simulator - asteroidal occultations

The light curve simulator provides a general simulator for asteroidal occultations. While it is particularly useful when the occulted star has a large diameter, it can be used for any occultation event.

The Simulator is mainly accessed via the form that displays the path of an occultation across the Earth - under the menu item Options... show Light Curve simulator. When accessed from here, the required details of the event are automatically passed to the simulator. It may also be accessed from the Occult main menu, Asteroid Predictions tab - where default values are used.

This is a plot of an occultation event involving a star with a large diameter: 2023 Dec 12, (319) Leona occulting Betelgeuse, using a preliminary ellipse for the expected orientation of the asteroid

[The Diameter and Limb Darkening of Betelgeuse have been taken from A&A Volume 572, December 2014, A17  Properties of.... Betelgeuse, and the axes of Leona from a preliminary shape model of this asteroid.]

The Across-path plot shows the light curve in a direction across the path. While this plot is always symmetric, its orientation is important for the Along-path light curve. For this purpose, the plot has the words LEFT and RIGHT near the bottom of the plot. These indicate the relevant side of the path when viewed along its direction of motion (which in this case is from East to West, such that RIGHT corresponds to the northern side of the path).

The Across-path plot has a red line which can be positioned using the slider under the plot. This is used to set the position of an observer for calculating the Along-path light curve plot. The number in that line just above the km scale is the distance from the center line, in km.

The Along-path plot is the expected light curve the observer would see from the across-path location set by the red line. When the asteroid is not represented as a circle, this light curve can become asymmetric, as shown in the above plot. This asymmetry is related to the orientation of the elongate asteroid relative to the path direction.

The possible shapes of the occultation curve are:

  • For the majority of occultations, the star diameter is effectively zero. As a result (and ignoring the effects of Fresnel diffraction) the light curve will drop almost vertically to the brightness of the asteroid, with a flat bottom - a Total occultation.
  • When the star has an appreciable apparent diameter, three types of occultations can occur:
    • If the star has an appreciable diameter, but is still smaller than the asteroid, the light curve will have a gradual decline and rise, with the central portion being at the asteroid light level (typically near zero). This is a Total occultation.
    • If the star has a diameter that is larger than the diameter of a circular asteroid, or the largest diameter of a non-circular asteroid, the light curve will have a gradual decline and rise, with the central portion being flat. This is an Annular occultation.  Importantly, when Limb Darkening is present/applied, the bottom of the central region will not be flat, but have a gentle downward curve.
    • for non-circular asteroids: if the major axis of the asteroid is larger than the star, and the minor axis is smaller, at maximum occultation on the center line the star disk will appear to  bisected by the asteroid (that is, with a crescents on both sides of the asteroid body). This is a Bisected occultation.
If the asteroid is non-circular (either an ellipse or a rectangle), the light curve in the Along-path direction is only symmetrical on the central line. At any other location across the path, maximum occultation does not occur exactly mid-way between the start and end of the event. This is illustrated in the above plot; the location on the Across-path light curve (as set by the slider/red-line) is at the RIGHT (the northern limit for this event) of the total occultation band, and the minimum of the Along-path light curve occurs later than the mid-time of the occultation at that location.

 

The plot features are:

Across path

Plot details:
  • Diameters of the star and asteroid, in mas, at top center
  • the percent light drop, and the magnitude it drops to (the magnitude of both the star and asteroid are included in these light drops)
  • An indication of the relevant side of the path, when setting the slider to generate the Along-path light curve

    Vertical scale :
  • Left side - illumination, as a fraction of full illumination
  • Right side - magnitude drop. Values larger than 3 are not tagged
  • The intervals indicated increase if the vertical scale of the plot is significantly increased.

    Horizontal scale :
  • Distance in km from the center line. Depending on whether distances as Topocentric is checked - the distance is on the Fundamental plane (unchecked), or on the Earth's surface (checked).
    • The intervals indicated increase if the vertical scale of the plot is significantly increased.

    Along path

    Plot details:
  • Diameters of the star and asteroid, in mas, at top center

    • Vertical scale :
  • Left side -illumination as a fraction of full illumination
  • Right side - magnitude drop. Values larger than 3 are not tagged
  • The intervals indicated increase if the vertical scale of the plot is significantly increased.

    Horizontal scale :
  • Time difference from mid-occultation, in seconds.
    • The intervals indicated increase if the vertical scale of the plot is significantly increased.


    Controls below the plot

    These check boxes control the parameters used to generate the plot. If the simulator is opened from an event plot, many of these parameters will be set (including the label above the plots). If it is opened from the Asteroid Predictions tab of Occult, default values will be set

    Plot parameters

  • Asteroid motion - mas/sec. Used to set the time scale in the Along path plot. This rate of motion is used to set the time scale on the Along-path plot. When called from the event Path plot, this field is automatically set and can't be changed.
  • Maximum duration This is a computed value, using the asteroid motion, and the major axis of the asteroid. For elliptic asteroids, the maximum duration occurs when the PA of the asteroid is 90° from the PA of the path.
  • show Light level lines. When checked, light lines are drawn across the plot at several light levels
  • show Magnitude lines. When checked, light lines are drawn across the plot at several magnitude drop levels
  • show Distance and Time lines. When checked, light  lines are drawn vertically across the plot at several distances (Across path) and times (Along path). The central line is emphasised.
  • distances as Topocentric When checked, the Across-Path distance scale is adjusted to give an approximate distance on the Earth's surface. When not checked, the distances are on the 'fundamental plane'

    • Star details

  • Diameter (mas) - the star's diameter. The minimum diameter that can be set is 0.1 mas.
  • Limb darkening. Values can be in the range of -0.10 to +0.80. When there is no limb darkening, the value should be set to 0. Very strong limb darkening would be 0.8. Limb darkening is most relevant to red stars (spectral types K & M), which are also the type of stars most likely to have a diameter sufficiently large for the effects of stellar diameter to be considered. For this reason, the default value of limb darkening is set at 0.05 (not 0)
  • Magnitude - the star's magnitude. Used with the asteroid's magnitude to set the magnitude drop taking into account the relative brightness of the two objects
    • . The magnitude type should be the same for both the star and asteroid - which usually means a V-magnitude should be used for the star.

    Asteroid details

  • Model : Ellipse or Rectangular. Options for the shape form to represent the profile of the asteroid at the event. Ellipse is most commonly applicable. Rectangular is intended for asteroids like Kleopatra or Eros. In any event, select a model type most appropriate to the asteroid profile at event time.
  • Major axis, km & mas. The diameter of a circle, or the major axis of an ellipse or rectangle, representing the asteroid. Most likely you will know the dimensions in km - and you should enter values on that basis. The value in mas represents the apparent size, and is used for the light curve calculations. The values are linked - such that changing one value will change the other value. IMPORTANT. The typical unit for the asteroid size is km; make sure you enter the km values into the left field. The right field - diameter in mas) - will be automatically populated.
  • Minor axis, km or mas. The diameter of a circle, or the minor axis of an ellipse or rectangle, representing the asteroid. Other details are as for the Major axis.
  • major axis PA° The position angle of the major axis of the ellipse or rectangle, referenced to the sky plane. This must be set manually.
  • PA of path The position angle of the path direction, referenced to the sky plane. This is set automatically.
  • Parallax - the asteroid's parallax. This is used to convert the asteroid diameters between mas and km. When called from the event Path plot, this field is automatically set and can't be changed.
  • Magnitude - the asteroid's magnitude. Used with the star's magnitude to compute the magnitude drop taking into account the relative brightness of the two objects.

    • Apart from setting these values to the most reliable values available, you can get an appreciation of the effects of the various parameters by changing them. For example: with an elliptical asteroid of similar diameter to the star, change the PA you can see how the light curve changes as the orientation of the asteroid is changed (hint - hold down the up or down button on the PA control to have it cycle through the full range of values.)

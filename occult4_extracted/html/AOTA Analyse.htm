AOTA: Tab 5. Analyse

 

Methodology

The detailed analysis of an event involves the fitting of a theoretical transition light curve to each of the transitions (the D and R events). Each transition is analysed separately.

The theoretical transition signals are based on a uniform light level before and after the transition, and a transition region of variable length. The transition region is calculated on the basis of the effects of stellar diameter. The effects of Fresnel diffraction are not included.

The default number of transition lengths that are investigated is 10. However the number can be varied between 5 and 30, using the UpDown box '# transitions'. The actual value used is non-critical - however the response time will increase when the number of transitions is large.

The analysis requires an identified region centered on the transition - which is where the detailed comparison of the light curves occur, and a region on either side to set the characteristics (mean and standard deviation) of the pre-and post-transition signals. These regions are coloured light yellow and light blue on the plot.

The analysis proceeds by comparing the theoretical transition signal for a given transition length with the observed signal and generated a χ2 statistic for each positioning of the theoretical signal along the measurement region of the observed signal. In this process the overall position of the theoretical signal remains fixed with respect to the observed signal; the variation is achieved by changing the positioning of the transition within the theoretical signal. The theoretical signal covers both the measurement region and the two regions on either side. However the transition region is moved only across the range of the measurement region.

The event time is identified with the location that has the minimum χ2 value. That value can be different for different length transitions. The uncertainties in the event times are estimated by way of a Monte Carlo analysis - on the basis of a user-selected transition length. The process involves:

  1. adding noise to one or other of the observed signal or the test signal (preferably, the noise is added to the test signal)
  2. performing the χ2 assessment of the event time - by determining the location for the minimum χ2 value for that particular noise-added situation
  3. Repeating (i) and (ii) a number of times; and
  4. Recording the differences between the minimums generated from each noise-added situation, and the original event time computed on the basis of no added noise.

Setting the regions

When the tab is entered from the previous step, the various regions required for the analysis are pre-set using default values. These values should be reviewed. The mean and standard deviations derived from the settings can be displayed on the plot by checking the Show measurement means checkbox in the center of the form (see figure below under The initial analysis, and plot information). The following plot shows the means (black line) and standard deviations (dashed green line at no occultation, dashed purple line during occultation) in the three circled areas.

During this part of the analysis, it is usually helpful to increase the plot scale - to 5 or 10 - so that you can view the detail of the transition region. [The program will automatically increase the plot scale when this tab is entered.]

The regions are identified with Yellow for the measurement region, and light blue for the before/after regions. Data points within those regions now have associated uncertainty bars - being derived from the standard deviation of the data points in the before/after regions.

Typically the uncertainty bars are smaller at the bottom of a light curve that at the top. By default the error bars are proportioned according to the Variance of the signals before and after the transition. However this may be changed to being proportioned according to standard deviation by checking the box 'Vary noise by Std Devn'.

Setting the regions incorrectly can have significant adverse effects on the analysis. The principles to follow are:

  • For a clean occultation event, have the start and end of the measurement measurements fairly close to the start and end of the observed light change (e.g. between 2 and 4 data points from the apparent start/end.
  • For events where the start and end of the transition are not well defined, make sure sufficient points are included in the measurement region to cover all possibilities of when the occultation occurred.
  • The regions on either side of the measurement region need to be sufficiently long so that they are a good representation of the signal. One indication is whether the mean line drawn through these data points is reasonable close to the dotted black line that represents the average used for the cross-correlation analysis.

The measurement regions are adjusted/set as follows.

  1. Ensure the radio button in the Adjust Regions group is set to 'Just before D'
  2. Place the cursor at the location where you think the measurement region for the D event should start, and click. The yellow region will be adjusted, and the location will appear in the text box next to the radio button.
  3. The radio button will have been automatically changed to 'Just after D'. Position the cursor at the desired location, and click.
  4. Repeat for the 'Just before R', and 'Just after R', clicking as required.

The extent of the side regions is adjusted using the 'Points outside of transition' UpDown box in the Adjust Regions group. This can be adjusted to being between 5 and 40.

Settings related to the χ2 analysis

The program fits multiple theoretical light curves to the observed light curve - differing by the duration (frames) over which the occultation transition occurs. The default setting is for 10 frames (plot points). However this can be adjusted to being between 5 and 30 frames (plot points) using the '# transitions' UpDown box in the χ2 analysis group.

In a typical occultation light curve, the noise at the bottom of the light curve is less than that at the top. For the χ2 analysis the noise needs to be appropriate to the signal level of each data point - which requires interpolating/extrapolating the noise level dependent upon the height of the data point. The default method for doing this is to linearly interpolate/extrapolate the noise on the basis of its Variance (variance is the square of the standard deviation). Checking the radio button 'Vary noise by Std Devn' will linearly interpolate/extrapolate the noise on the basis of its Standard Deviation.

Settings related to the Monte Carlo analysis

Number of trials

The uncertainty in the event time is estimated using a Monte Carlo analysis. The process involves taking the original signal (either the observed signal, or the trial signal), adding noise, and computing the event time for that noise-affected situation. This process is repeated a large number of times (trials) to obtain a statistically reliable indication of the range of possible solutions given the level of noise present.

The number of trials that are conducted is set by the UpDown box '# Monte Carlo trials' in the Monte Carlo Parameters group. The default setting is 200, but this can be changed to between 100 and 2000. Note that when the number of trial is large, the program responsiveness becomes slow.

Application of Noise

When running the Monte Carlo trials, there are two options for applying the noise:

  1. Apply the noise as additional noise to the measured light signal - and fit a 'pure' test signal to this doubly-noise-affected signal; or
  2. Apply the noise to the test signal - such that a noise affected test signal is fitted to the observed (& therefore noise-affected) measured light signal.

The default setting is to apply the noise to the test signal. This can be changed to add the extra noise to the observed signal by checking the radio button 'Noise applied to measured' {signal} in the Monte Carlo Parameters group.

Noise limitations

The noise signal that is added uses a random number generator with a normal distribution. [The algorithm used is the Box-Muller algorithm]. This will generate numbers that potentially are many standard deviations away from the mean. Large noise deviations can be excluded from the noise applied in the Monte Carlo analysis by adjusting the setting in the UpDown box labelled Std Dev limit on noise' in the Monte Carlo Parameters group. The default setting is 3; it can be varied between 2 and 4.

The distribution of the possible event times appear to frequently have significant differences associated with whether the Monte Carlo noise is added to the observed signal or the test signal. It is believed that the results obtained when adding the noise to the test signal are more reliable.

The initial analysis, and plot information

Click the button 'Get event locations' to conduct the initial analysis. The following data and plot information is generated - on the basis that the occultation transition occurred in a single frame:

  • Below that button, the frame identification for each transition. That frame number is either a whole number or ends in 0.5. Two frames will be given - corresponding to the start and end of the event uncertainty region. Additionally, the length of the transition from full light to occulted light for the fitted light curve is given.
  • In the two plots under the Heading 'χ2 for different transitions', plots of the minimum χ2 value for each transition duration assessed. [The plot is actually inverted, such that the highest bar corresponds to the least χ2 value]. In this plot the bar that has been filled-in green corresponds to the transition length used for the uncertainty analysis. The numbers on the bars indicate the length of the transition region for that bar.
  • In the two plots under the Heading 'Uncertainty range', plots of the distribution of solutions for the event time arising from the Monte Carlo trials. Each bar in these plots indicates the relative number of event solutions, with the horizontal axis giving the frame (plot point) offset of the trial solution from the 'original' solution. The range of offset values is -9 to +9 frames. Anything that appears in the +10/-10 position corresponds to offsets of greater or equal to 10, and are coloured red. As a general proposition, any solution that has data in this region is likely to be unreliable or invalid. Occasionally the extreme bars in the distribution will be colored orange. This will occur when the total count of events included in that bar (plus any bars at larger values) is less than 5% of the total number of trials. Any such bars can be disregarded when assessing the uncertainty range.
  • On the plot of the event, the occultation curve is plotted - in a pinkish-red. The limits of the uncertainty range in the location of the occultation curve are plotted as a red dashed line - to facilitate an understanding of the cause of the uncertainty when it is more than a frame or two. The top and bottom of the light curve is positioned according to the signal heights in the regions on either side of the measurement region. The slope of the occultation curve is plotted in accordance with the selected transition - see below. Also plotted (but in faint black dotted line) is the square-wave occultation signal fitted from the cross-correlation analysis.
  • Also plotted (in green) is the line representing the variation of χ2 over the measurement region. The event time corresponds to the minimum of this curve. The 'flatness' of this curve is an indicator of how well the event time is defined.
  • Optionally, the cross-correlation curve can be plotted, by checking cross-corrl checkbox in the purple region.
  • Optionally, the mean light levels used for the theoretical light curve can be plotted by checking meas. means checkbox in the purple region.
  • Optionally, the error bars associated with each data point near the measurement region can be plotted by checking error bars checkbox in the purple region.

Investigating different transition durations

The effect of different transitions is ascertained using χ2 for different transitions plot. This can be used as follows - with the D and R events being treated separately

  1. Click the cursor on the region labelled 'ALL'. That region will now be coloured orange. The χ2 curves for all transitions will now appear on the main plot
  2. Click the cursor on the first box. That box will appear Green, to indicate it has been selected. The plot will show the χ2 curve for that transition (a transition over a single frame). The event time displayed under 'Location of occultation' will be for that selected transition duration. Also plotted in the Uncertainty range is the distribution of event solutions - with the zero-point being the solution for the selected transition duration.
  3. Click the cursor on a different bar in the 'χ2 for different transitions' plot. This will recalculate the solution and uncertainties on the basis of the selected transition duration, with the results being displayed as before. Note also that the plot of the occultation light curve and χ2 curve will also be updated.

Interpreting the results - Which transition duration to use

For an event with instantaneous step events, the χ2 evaluation of the time of the event is completely insensitive to the length of the transition used in the test signal. Consequently constancy in the heights of the χ2 bars in the bar chart are indicative of a step event. The heights of the bars in the χ2 plot only have significant variation when the observed signal clearly involves transition duration.

For guidance on how to select a relevant transition:

  • If the plot of 'χ2 for different transitions' has little variation, or has a general slow increase or decrease in height - analyse the observation on the basis of a single transition
  • Only use a larger transition if the plot of 'χ2 for different transitions' first increases, then decreases, with the highest point in the plot being significantly higher (>15%?) than the bar for transition #1.
  • Keep an eye on the extent of the uncertainty as between adjacent transition bars. If appropriate select the transition that results in the smaller uncertainty range. HOWEVER remember that the uncertainty range is derived from a Monte Carlo analysis, and different runs of the Monte Carlo will result in slightly different results. So click the selected transition bar several times and note the subsequent changes in the distribution of the uncertainty results.
  • There can be systematic differences between odd- and even- numbered transitions. This is usually an indication that the event occurs either at the start of a frame, or half-way though a frame. In such cases select the higher of two adjacent bars.

Interpreting the uncertainty range plot

The plot of the uncertainty range is a bar-chart plot of the distribution of results from the Monte Carlo analysis. The sum height of all the bars equals the height of the plot. When an event is very well defined, the plot will show a single bar, with perhaps a very small adjacent bar. Frequently the event will be well defined but 'between' two frames - resulting in two adjacent bars of similar height. These two situations are shown in the R and D plots (respectively) in the following:

When an event is poorly defined, the plot will have numerous bars spread across the plot. When there is a column at the left or right end of the plot (as in the D example in the next plot), you can assume that the event is quite unreliable.

Sometimes the observation is such that there is more than one possible solution. This will be evidenced by two (or more) groups of bars with gaps between the groups - as in the Reappearance example in the following plot. These situations require careful inspection to be sure the possibilities are real. However note that it would be erroneous to try to prevent the situation by 'judicious' setting the measurement regions to avoid 'inconvenient' parts of the light curve!



Changing the confidence level

The uncertainties computed for the event times are based on a level of confidence that the event occurred within the specified uncertainty range. The default level of confidence is 90% - and that level is set whenever AOTA is run.

The Uncertainty is calculated on the basis of the Monte Carlo trials. That is, with a level of confidence set at 90%, 90% of the Monte Carlo trials result in an event time that is within the uncertainty limits.

There may be circumstances where the user believes the uncertainty range is too broad in the circumstances. A smaller uncertainty range can be generated by changing the level of confidence:

Separate levels of confidence are available for both the D and R events. The levels of confidence used will be included in the report generated by AOTA.

As a general principle, reducing the level of confidence in order to reduce the uncertainties in the event time is not appropriate. Where a level of confidence less than 90% is used in the analysis, this fact should be included in the report of the observations.



Disregarding data points

Occasionally the assessment of the location of an event can be adversely affected by the presence of an anomalous data point near the event. When such a data point is removed, the solution goes from having a large uncertainty to a small uncertainty. This is illustrated in the following example. A point can be disregarded by pointing the cursor to the location of the point, hold down the CTRL key and Right-Click the mouse. [This is noted above the AOTA plot.] This will set the data point as being invalid - appearing as a tick on the axis at the bottom of the plot. It data point can be restored by repeating the CTRL + Right-Click.

Disregarding data points should not be done as a matter of routine. There needs to be good reason to disregard a data point. Relevant considerations are:

  1. Does disregarding of a single data point make a large difference in the solution? If so, this could suggest the data point has too great an effect on the solution, which is an indication that it might be appropriate to disregard the data point.
  2. Does the plot of the uncertainty range indicate two discrete solutions, or a broad band of uncertainty? Disregarding a data point is generally not appropriate when the uncertainty plot shows a broad range. In this example the uncertainty plot with frame 596 present (the upper plot) has two separated peaks - indicating that it may be appropriate to disregard a data point.
  3. Is the uncertainty solution asymmetric? The dotted red line in the fitted light curve shows the most-likely location of the event, while the solid lines show the uncertainty limits. Where one uncertainty limit is much greater than the other, the possibility of this being caused by a single errant data point needs to be considered.
  4. What does a visual inspection of the relevant frame(s) of the video show? In this example, inspection showed the star was clearly present at frames 598-600, thereby establishing the D did not occur at frame 596. This gave clear support to disregarding frame 596.

When data points are disregarded in the region of an event, or there are invalid data points in that region, the number of such data points within 10 points of the transition will be included in the report generated by AOTA. As a general principle, the more disregarded or invalid data points in the region, the less reliable the result.

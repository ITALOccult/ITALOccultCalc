Introduction

The civilian time scale UT is defined by the rotation of the Earth about its axis. However the Earth's rotation is non-constant, slowing down by an irregular amount, whereas the expressions for the motions of the planets use a strictly constant time scale. Consequently, to compute the position of astronomical objects in the sky, it is necessary to correct Universal Time (which is measured by the earth's slowing rotation) to obtain Terrestrial Dynamical Time (a strictly uniform time scale based on Time Atomique International). For ancient times, this correction can be as large as several hours.

It needs to be remembered that for computing the position of an observer on the Earth's surface relative to the center of the Earth, the appropriate time scale to use is UT - which can differ from UTC by up to 0.9secs. Additionally, for accurate analysis it is necessary to correct site coordinates for polar motion.

Further below is a summary of the various time scales, from the US Naval Observatory.

In the following, the term ΔT is used loosely to refer to the difference between UT (or UTC) and the time scale of ephemerides

Application of corrections

Prior to 1962, all time measurements were made using the rotational position of the Earth as the primary determinant of time. Coordinated Universal Time, based upon an atomic time standard rather than the rotational position of the Earth, began in 1962. UTC compares to true Universal time in the following way:
  • Between 1962 and 1972 - constant small corrections were applied, so that the broadcast time signal UTC was very close to Universal Time
  • From 1972, the broadcast time signal UTC is kept within 0.9s of true Universal Time with an adjustment by exactly 1 second (a leap second). The difference from UT (in tenths of a second) is included as code in the broadcast signal.

The value of ΔT used in Occult is based based on the Moon's orbital tidal deceleration being -25.82"/cy2. This is the value used in DE430 and DE440. Uncertainty in ΔT

For dates in modern times, and for a few years into the future, the uncertainty in ΔT is usually less than 1 second. Indeed, from 1950 to current dates, it is less than 0.1 secs.

However for historical events, or for dates in the future, the uncertainty can become significant. Indeed for dates prior to -9200, or after 10700, the uncertainty exceeds a full revolution of the Earth - making the conversion between TT and UT totally meaningless in any practical sense.

The uncertainty in the values of ΔT are incorporated from the tables downloaded (as above) from here (UK Nautical Almanac Office)

Estimates of the uncertainty in ΔT are provided for predictions of solar eclipses, lunar eclipses, and transits.

 

Summary of Time Scales

The following, from the US Naval Observatory, sets out the main time scales. For most practical purposes, the time scales of interest are UTC (the broadcast time signal) and TDT (the time argument for ephemerides)

  • Atomic Time , with the unit of duration the Systeme International (SI) second defined as the duration of 9,192,631,770 cycles of radiation corresponding to the transition between two hyperfine levels of the ground state of cesium 133. TAI is the International Atomic Time scale, a statistical timescale based on a large number of atomic clocks.
  • Universal Time (UT) is counted from 0 hours at midnight, with unit of duration the mean solar day, defined to be as uniform as possible despite variations in the rotation of the Earth.
    • UT0 is the rotational time of a particular place of observation. It is observed as the diurnal motion of stars or extraterrestrial radio sources.
    • UT1 is computed by correcting UT0 for the effect of polar motion on the longitude of the observing site. It varies from uniformity because of the irregularities in the Earth's rotation.
  • Coordinated Universal Time (UTC) differs from TAI by an integral number of seconds. UTC is kept within 0.9 seconds of UT1 by the introduction of one-second steps to UTC, the "leap second." To date these steps have always been positive.
  • Dynamical Time replaced ephemeris time as the independent argument in dynamical theories and ephemerides. Its unit of duration is based on the orbital motions of the Earth, Moon, and planets.
    • Terrestrial Time (TT), (or Terrestrial Dynamical Time, TDT), with unit of duration 86400 SI seconds on the geoid, is the independent argument of apparent geocentric ephemerides. TDT = TAI + 32.184 seconds.
    • Barycentric Dynamical Time (TDB), is the independent argument of ephemerides and dynamical theories that are referred to the solar system barycenter. TDB varies from TT only by periodic variations.

ΔT is the difference between Earth rotational time (UT1) and dynamical time (TDT).
ΔTA is the difference between UTC and TAI.
The difference between UTC and TAI is ΔTA + 32.184s.

Assessing the correction to ΔT using Lunar Occultation results

In principle the quantity ΔT represents the difference in time between a uniformly rotating Earth, and the actual rotating Earth. Prior to 1961, all time measurements were based on the rotating Earth. 1961 saw the commencement of atomic clocks for precise time keeping.

The primary means for determining the value of ΔT for ancient epochs has been from a comparison of solar eclipse observations. For the period 1700 to 1960, the primary determinant of ΔT has been lunar occultations - working on the principal that given a reliable lunar ephemeris, the mean residuals in longitude should come out as zero.

When determining ΔT using lunar occultations, it is important to recognise that the accuracy is affected by:

  • the overall accuracy of the lunar ephemeris
  • the effects of personal equation (as almost all lunar occultations were recorded visually)
  • the source of time used for the observation
  • the adopted value of the tidal acceleration of the moon
  • the reference frame used for the star positions - including the values used for precession
  • the basis of the proper motions of the stars

Process for listing the ΔT corrections

The following steps are required. Note that Administrator access must be set to enable this functionality.
  • The values are specific the the DE version used for the ephemerides. If necessary, download and install the latest DE version.
  • Click the Generate the Vizier Archive' button above this section of the form. This creates the Vizier Archive file. It will take several hours, depending on computing speed. If this file does not exist, the functionality below will not be enabled.
  • Under the box for ΔT correction - from VizieR Archive click Get new values. This will read the Vizier Archivefile and derive new values of ΔT. The data is assessed in bins of odd numbers of years (7 years before 1750, 5 years between 1750 and 1800, 3 years between 1800 and 1830, 1 year thereafter). The values displayed are:
    • the Mean year value for the bin
    • The mean value of δT used for the observations in that bin
    • The correction (in seconds) to be added to the used ΔT value
    • the Standard Deviation of that correction (in seconds)
    • the number of observations used
    The data in this table can be copied using Copy displayed list.
To exclude observations affected by errors in timing, star identification and site location, the observations used to evaluate ΔT are limited to those where the residual is less than 3 standard deviations from the mean. The observations in each bin are iterated 10 times. In each iteration, observations more than 3 Standard deviations from the mean of the previous iteration are excluded.  In practice, the the number of iterations to obtain a stable solution was in the order of 3.

Additionally, observations are excluded under the following circumstances:

  • The event is flagged as one of - unknown or invalid site location, unidentified star, invalid observation, or unresolved errors;
  • The event type is one of Miss, Start, End, Other or Missing data;
  • The rate of motion of the star normal to the lunar limb, as a ratio of the rate of motion of the moon, is less than 0.5 (before 1800), 0.6 (1800 to 1830) or 0.7 (after 1830)

Header block

Line #Meaning
1This line gives the star number, and the Visual and Red magnitudes
2This optional line gives alternative names for the star. The names can be proper names, Bayer or Flamsteed identifiers. Only given if the star is brighter than mag 5.
3Gives the date of the event. This line gives the date hour and minute of the first and last point in the prediction. The date will be different if the event extends over a day boundary.
5Nominal site altitude. To minimise the need to adjust a prediction for the effect of altitude, the prediction can be generated at a 'nominal' altitude representative of altitudes in the likely region for observing the graze.
7, 8Header text for the prediction.


Path prediction

ColumnMeaning
E LongitudeGives the longitude of a point on the path. The default format is 'd m s'. But you can change the format to 'd mm.m' in the User Settings option under the Maintenance tab - under 'Lunar Occultations - Grazes'.

In most circumstances the longitude increases through the prediction. HOWEVER in certain circumstances a graze path will cross a line of longitude at two different latitudes. This will only occur near the beginning or end of a graze path, and only at relatively high latitudes with the moon at a low altitude. When this occurs, the relevant end of the graze path will have a portion where the longitude decreases.
LatitudeLatitude of the graze path at the given longitude - in same format as for longitude.
UTThe UT of mid-graze at tat latitude/longitude. For the day, check line 3 of the header.
Sun AltAltitude of the sun (in deg). Only given if the altitude is higher than -9 deg.
Moon AltAltitude of the moon at mid-graze
Moon AzAzimuth of the moon at mid-graze
TanZA factor used to adjust the location of the graze path for the site's altitude. If the site altitude is H meters higher than the nominal site altitude used to generate the prediction, the path should moved in the direction of the moon by the distance TanZ * H meters.

Note that quite often the lateral displacement of the path can be quite small. However observers should be alert to the possibility that the correction is large, in which case it may be best to regenerate the prediction for the actual site altitude.

IMPORTANT. The graze prediction generated by Occult is fully corrected for the effects of atmospheric refraction at low altitudes.

PAThe Position Angle of the point of mid-graze. PA is measured eastwards around the limb of the moon, from true north.
AAThe Axis Angle of the point of mid-graze. AA is measured eastwards around the limb of the moon, from the lunar north pole.
CAThe angle from the nearest cusp of the moon. 'N' and 'S' indicate which cusp the angle is measured from. Negative values indicate the event is against the bright limb.

If the graze occurs during a lunar eclipse, this column gives the distance of the star from the center of the umbra using a range of 0 to 105 - where 100 is the umbral radius.

Note - all path coordinates are referred to WGS84 (as used by GPS), with site altitudes being referenced to Mean Sea Level.

Grazes of the planets, and the moons of Jupiter and Saturn.

When the graze is of a major planet or the moons of Jupiter or Saturn, two graze paths are generated. The first path is preceded with text like:
Inner limit - object fully obscured
while the second path is preceded with text like
Outer limit - object just touches the moon

Between these two lines the planet or moon will be partially obscured by the moon. In computing these lines, no allowance is made for the oblateness of Jupiter and Saturn, nor for the rings of Saturn.


Graze profile

The graze profile varies quite significantly along the path. For this reason a graze profile is not automatically generated for a manual prediction. Rather, right-click on a line of interest, and select 'Graze profile [at selected line]'.

Visibility table

The visibility table indicates the faintest star that will be visible for a given combination of telescope aperture (Tdia) and cusp angle (CA). Five values of CA are given, with the middle row corresponding to mid-graze.

Experience has shown that the values given in this table are fairly reliable for a range of telescope sizes and observer experiences. Generally speaking, if the star is more than a magnitude brighter than the table indicates for the particular telescope/CA combination, the graze will be fairly easy to observe in 'ordinary' conditions. Experienced observers in good conditions will usually be able to observe to the limiting magnitudes given in the table.

The visibility of a graze can vary significantly along the graze path due to changes in the cusp angle, and the altitude of the moon. The table generated with the prediction is for a point mid-way along the computed path (and the location is indicated above the table). However the table can be generated for any point along the table. Right-click on a line of interest, and select 'Limiting magnitude table [at selected line]'

Double star catalogue details

If the star is flagged as a double star, the full details from the Washington Double Star catalogue, and the 4th Interferometric catalogue, will be listed here.


Observer scan

Note: The effectiveness of the Observer Scan depends upon the user creating site files containing all observers in a region.

The final part of the prediction is a list of sites where the graze passes nearby. The basis for inclusion in this list is:

  • All sites in the site file used for the prediction are considered;
  • A site is listed if the graze passes within the graze travel distance for that site (using the values held in that site file).
The date provided are
  • Site name, and its latitude/longitude
  • the minimum distance to the graze path; and
  • the UT of the time of closest approach
